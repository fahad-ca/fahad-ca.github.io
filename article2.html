60% of Pakistani Marriages Are Between Cousins. Here Is What That Costs the Children.

A comparative epidemiological analysis of childhood disability and birth defects across countries with and without consanguineous marriage, and what the real numbers reveal about Pakistan's hidden public health burden.

Fahad Naveed

Fintech | CA Finalist | ICAP | Ex-Audit Intern

60-70% of all Pakistani marriages are between blood relatives
2x the birth defect risk for first-cousin children vs. unrelated parents
5-6M children in Pakistan affected by thalassemia, fuelled by cousin marriage
3.1% Pakistan's official disability figure. The reality is vastly higher.

This is not a comfortable article to write. And it is not a comfortable one to read. But it is a necessary one.

Between 10% and 20% of all people on Earth live in communities where marrying a close relative is not just accepted but actively encouraged. In Pakistan, roughly 60% to 70% of all marriages take place between blood relatives. Of those, first-cousin marriages alone account for exactly half. This makes Pakistan the country with the highest rate of consanguineous marriage on the planet.

The consequences for children born from these unions are measurable, documented, and severe. Yet the practice continues, the data goes uncollected, and the children carry the weight of choices made before they were born.

The Global Picture

A Practice More Common Than Most People Know

Consanguineous marriage is structurally defined as a reproductive union between individuals who are closely related by blood, typically second cousins or closer. It is a deeply entrenched demographic and cultural phenomenon that shapes the genetic, epidemiological, and socioeconomic structure of numerous global populations.

While global shifts, increasing education, and market-driven economies have led to a rapid decline in consanguinity across Western and East Asian regions, the practice remains highly prevalent and culturally resilient in the Middle East, North Africa, and South Asia.

From a public health perspective, this commands serious scientific attention because of its established correlation with childhood disability, congenital anomalies, early infant mortality, and rare recessive genetic disorders.

The Biology

How Shared DNA Becomes a Health Risk

When two people who share a common ancestor have children, those children are more likely to inherit two identical copies of the same harmful gene variant, one from each parent. This is the core biological mechanism through which a genetic condition that would normally stay dormant gets expressed and causes real disease.

How Recessive Genes Work

Consider a single harmful recessive gene, shown as "a." A healthy carrier has one normal copy (A) and one harmful copy (a). When two carriers have a child together, each child faces a 25% chance of inheriting both harmful copies (aa) and expressing the disease. In first-cousin marriages, the probability that both parents are carriers of the same gene is dramatically elevated, because they inherited DNA from the same grandparents.

The human genome has approximately 3,500 distinct recessive conditions. On average, every person carries about 12 silent mutations linked to these conditions. In a couple of unrelated people, the chance both carry a mutation in the same gene is very small. In a first-cousin couple, that chance is multiplied fourfold compared to second cousins.

Why the doubling matters at scale: A 3-percentage-point increase sounds small individually. Pakistan records roughly 5 million births per year. With 60-70% of parents being related, that is approximately 3.5 million consanguineous births annually. A 3-point higher defect rate means around 105,000 additional children born with genetic conditions every single year, compared to a purely outbred population.

The Social Context

Why Do People Continue to Marry Cousins?

Understanding the health risks does not explain why cousin marriage persists. To understand that, we have to look at the economic and social systems that make it not just acceptable, but strategically rational for millions of families.

Intensive Kinship: The Family as Safety Net

In agrarian and economically vulnerable societies, marrying within the family is a rational survival strategy. It keeps land and inheritance within the household. It creates working partnerships between households. It lowers dowry costs and builds social alliances within clans and castes that offer real protection and mutual support. Studies of communities in Kabirwala, Punjab, confirm this pattern has deep roots.

Extensive Kinship: The State as Safety Net

In industrialized societies, formal institutions like banks, insurance, and state welfare replace the family as the primary safety net. People move for work and education, meet partners outside their family circle, and depend on the state rather than relatives. Marriage outside the family fits naturally into this structure, making cousin marriage both unnecessary and socially unusual.

The day-to-day economic and social benefits of cousin marriage are real and immediate. The genetic risks are statistical and happen in a minority of families. People weigh what they can see and feel against what feels distant and abstract. This is not a failure of morality. It is a predictable response to economic constraints.

The Law

What Different Countries Actually Do About It

Legal frameworks shape more than marriage practices. They determine whether governments track health consequences, fund screening programs, and whether communities can discuss risks openly.

Pakistan

Fully Legal

Cousin marriage is entirely legal, culturally encouraged, and completely unrestricted by the state. Health ministries attempt mitigation through premarital genetic counseling, but face severe resource deficits, low genetic literacy, and deep cultural resistance.

60-70% of all marriages are consanguineous

United States

Mixed by State

A highly fragmented legal landscape. Banned in 25 states, restricted in 7, and criminalized with jail time in 8 states, making the US one of the few countries to treat cousin marriage as a criminal offence.

China

National Ban

A national ban enforced since the 1980s and 1990s. State documents explicitly cited the prevention of birth defects and intellectual disabilities. The ban has virtually eliminated the practice and fundamentally altered China's genetic health baseline.

National Ban

Firm legal bans rooted in centuries of anti-inbreeding cultural norms, now encoded in civil law. South Korea operates the KNDRS (Korea National Disability Registration System), providing highly accurate epidemiological data unclouded by widespread inbreeding.

The Thalassemia Burden: 5 to 6 Million Affected

Pakistan faces a systemic burden of hemoglobin disorders. An estimated 5 to 6 million children live with thalassemia or its severe symptoms. Approximately 5,000 homozygous thalassemia children are born annually, a direct biological consequence of the 6% of the national population carrying the recessive gene, relentlessly amplified through cousin marriages.

Source: Exploring the role of consanguinity in thalassemia prevalence in Pakistan, PMC 2024.

The Definitive Study

Born in Bradford: The Study That Separated Genetics from Poverty

One of the most scientifically important studies on consanguinity comes not from Pakistan but from Bradford, UK. The Born in Bradford (BiB) study tracked 13,500 babies born at Bradford Royal Infirmary between 2007 and 2011. Bradford has a large Pakistani-origin community, many of whom maintain the tradition of cousin marriage despite living in the UK for one or two generations.

This setting was scientifically invaluable precisely because it controlled for the environmental factors that usually accompany high consanguinity in Pakistan: poverty, poor sanitation, inadequate healthcare, and malnutrition. Pakistani-origin families in Bradford had high rates of cousin marriage, but they lived within the NHS and the UK welfare system.

The genetic variable was present. The environmental confounders were not. When researchers tested whether poverty explained the higher defect rates, they found it did not. Two-thirds of the Bradford cohort lived in the UK's most deprived areas, yet socioeconomic deprivation did not statistically explain the surge. Cousin marriage was the mathematically dominant factor.

Other Factors

What Makes Things Better or Worse

Consanguinity does not work alone. Several other factors amplify or reduce its effects on child health, and understanding them is essential for designing realistic responses.

Poverty, Nutrition and Maternal Age

In Pakistan, genetic risks collide with widespread poverty. Over 40% of children experience multidimensional poverty. Poor maternal nutrition amplifies the risk of neural tube defects like anencephaly and spina bifida, which are partly preventable through folic acid supplementation. The US, China, and South Korea mandate food fortification. Pakistan does not have a comparable national program.

Maternal age also matters differently across contexts. Western countries see more chromosomal disorders like Down syndrome from older mothers. Pakistan's disability burden is skewed toward inherited recessive conditions because mothers are younger, not toward the chromosomal aneuploidies tied to aging eggs.

Newborn Screening and Prenatal Diagnosis

The US, China, and South Korea run newborn screening programs that routinely test babies for dozens of metabolic and genetic conditions. Early detection prevents anomalies from progressing into lifelong disabilities. Children with congenital hypothyroidism, identified immediately after birth, can live completely normal lives. Without early detection, the same condition causes severe intellectual disability.

Pakistan does not have a formal national newborn screening program. Research shows a gap of 2.9 to 4.1 years between the time parents first notice signs of intellectual disability in their child and the time they actually seek medical care, meaning children miss rehabilitation during their most critical years of brain development.

The Cultural Barrier to Genetic Counseling

In communities where cousin marriage is the norm, telling people the practice is risky feels like an attack on their family, their religion, and their identity. Focus group research among Pakistani-origin communities in Luton, UK, found significant resistance to medical advice on this topic. Participants did not dismiss the evidence, but they asked for information that acknowledged their community's values and framing.

This tells us that public health messages must be delivered with cultural intelligence, not just clinical authority. Telling people their traditions are harmful is not an effective health intervention. Giving them tools to protect their children within their own value framework is.

The Data Paradox

Why Pakistan's Official Numbers Tell the Wrong Story

Pakistan's official 2023 Census reported a disability prevalence of 3.10%. The United States reports 8.56% for developmental disabilities in children. This seems impossible given everything we know about consanguinity, poverty, and poor healthcare in Pakistan. How can a country with such serious health problems report lower disability than the United States?

The answer is that Pakistan's figure is almost certainly a massive undercount. The paradox is entirely an artifact of how disability is measured, who gets counted, and who is allowed to be visible.

Pakistan's Method: Binary Yes/No

Historical census data asked simple yes-or-no questions using outdated terminology. "Are you blind? Crippled? Mentally retarded?" This only captures the most severe, physically obvious disabilities. Mild autism, learning difficulties, moderate physical impairments, and hundreds of other conditions simply do not exist in the official data.

3.1% Result: a massive, systematic undercount

WHO Method: Functional Difficulty Scale

The Washington Group Short Set (WG-SS) does not ask if you "are disabled." It asks: do you have difficulty seeing? Difficulty hearing? Difficulty concentrating? Answers are measured on a gradient from no difficulty to cannot do at all. This captures vastly more of the population living with functional limitations.

~15% WHO global disability estimate for comparison

Who Gets Hidden, and Why

The Hidden Child

In traditional societies built on intensive kinship, disability carries profound social stigma. A child born with a visible defect or intellectual disability may be seen as a source of family shame. Families may keep such children out of sight when census workers arrive. The child may never be registered with any medical or social service.

The Double Discrimination of Girls

This is especially severe for female children. In communities where marriage is the primary pathway for women, having a disabled daughter can be seen as making her unmarriageable. Families have a strong incentive to conceal a daughter's disability entirely. She never gets counted. The effect is especially acute because girls and women are already underrepresented in health data across South Asia.

The Delayed Diagnosis

Research shows a gap of 2.9 to 4.1 years between the time parents first notice signs of intellectual disability and the time they seek medical care. This means many cases are never formally diagnosed at all, let alone entered into any official registry, let alone counted in a census.

The Way Forward

What Needs to Change

The path forward does not require prohibition alone. The evidence from permissive jurisdictions suggests that legal bans are not the primary lever in deeply consanguineous societies. What is needed is a combination of structural healthcare reform, better data collection, and culturally intelligent communication.

  • Scale Up Genetic Counseling and Prenatal Screening Premarital and prenatal genetic screening programs need to be genuinely accessible in rural and semi-urban areas. Couples need to know their carrier status before they have children, not after. This requires trained genetic counselors, subsidized testing, and healthcare centers equipped to refer patients appropriately.
  • Establish a National Newborn Screening Program Pakistan does not have a formal national newborn screening program. China, South Korea, and the US screen every newborn for dozens of metabolic and genetic conditions. Early detection of treatable conditions prevents permanent disability. This is not a luxury. It is a basic healthcare infrastructure requirement.
  • Adopt WHO-Standard Disability Measurement Pakistan needs to fully adopt the Washington Group Short Set on Functioning across all national surveys and the census. Without accurate data, there can be no accurate policy. The current 3.1% figure makes the problem invisible to policymakers. That invisibility has a cost measured in children's lives.
  • Deliver Culturally Intelligent Health Communication Focus group research shows Pakistani communities do not dismiss genetic risk evidence. They reject the way it is communicated when it feels like an attack on their identity. Public health messaging must be designed with community leaders, religious scholars, and families, not against them. Telling people their traditions are harmful is not a health intervention. Giving them tools to protect their children within their own value framework is.
Conclusion

The Evidence Is Clear. The Silence Has a Price.

The comparative epidemiology across these jurisdictions reveals patterns that are hard to explain in any other way than through the biological effects of genetic homozygosity. Children born to first cousins face roughly double the baseline risk of congenital anomalies. Applied across a country of 240 million people where the majority of marriages are between cousins, that doubling generates a public health burden that is vast and persistent.

The Born in Bradford study provided the most rigorous evidence: even with access to the UK's healthcare system, good nutrition, and no extreme poverty, Pakistani-origin children born to cousin parents still showed double the birth defect rate. The genetics were not cancelled out by better living conditions.

Countries that have banned cousin marriage do not show the same pattern of recessive genetic disease at a population level. Their disability burdens exist but are driven by entirely different factors.

Pakistan's official 3.10% disability figure does not reflect reality. It reflects the limits of who gets counted, who is allowed to be visible, and which suffering is socially permitted to exist.

The difference in childhood disability rates between permissive and restricted jurisdictions is not just a law-and-genetics story. It is a story about economics, cultural values, medical infrastructure, and the willingness to honestly measure hard problems.

The children born into these circumstances did not choose the genetic odds they were given. The adults around them, and the systems those adults build, determine whether those odds are acknowledged, mitigated, or silently passed on to the next generation.
